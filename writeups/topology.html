Notes on Topology

Compact Spaces

Definition. A compact space is one for which every (open) cover has a finite subcover.

Proposition. A closed subspace of a compact space is compact.

Proof. Let X be a compact space, and let A be closed in X. Let A_j, with j indexing over an index set J be a (open) covering of A. For each j, since A_j is open in A, there exists an open set B_j such that A_j is equal to the intersection of A with B_j. The family B_j does not necessarily cover X. However, if we append the open set X - A to this family, then it does cover X. Since X is compact, there is a finite subfamily C_k, where k indexes over the positive integers 1 to n, of this family that covers X. Consider the finite subfamily constructed by taking A intersect C_k for each k such that C_k is not X - A. Each of these is an element of the original family A_j, and their union is A. Thus, A is compact.
