Notes on Topology

Compact Spaces

Definition. A compact space is one for which every (open) cover has a finite subcover.

Proposition. A closed subspace of a compact space is compact.

Proof. Let X be a compact space, and let A be closed in X. Let A_j, with j indexing over an index set J, be a (open) cover of A. For each j, since A_j is open in A, there exists an open set B_j such that A_j is equal to the intersection of A with B_j. The family B_j does not necessarily cover X. However, if we append the open set X - A to this family, then it does cover X. Since X is compact, there is a finite subfamily C_k, where k indexes over the positive integers 1 to n, of this family that covers X. Consider the finite subfamily constructed by taking A intersect C_k for each k such that C_k is not X - A. Each of these is an element of the original family A_j, and their union is A. Thus, A is compact.

Proposition. A compact subspace of a Hausdorff space is closed.

Proof. Let X be Hausdorff and A be a compact subspace of X. We show that the complement of A, X - A, is open. Consider a point b of X - A. For each point a in A, there exists disjoint (open) neighborhoods U_a of a and V_a of b. The family consisting of A intersected with U_a for each a in A is a cover of A; since A is compact, it has a finite subcover consisting of elements of the form A intersected with U_{a_k}, where k indexes over the positive integers 1 to n. Now, consider the finite intersection of neighborhoods V_{a_k} of b; it is a neighborhood of b that is disjoint from A (note that A is contained in the union of U_{a_k}, which is disjoint from the intsection of V_{a_k}) and thus is contained in X - A.
